The Sparsest Cut Problem
Flows, Metrics & Embeddings

A self-contained deep dive into one of the most fundamental problems in combinatorial optimization — finding the “best bang-per-buck” cut in a capacitated graph.

■ Best ratio: \(O(\sqrt{\log n})\) [ARV 2004] ■ Flow-cut gap: \(\Theta(\log k)\)

§1 Introduction & Motivation

Imagine you manage a data-center network. Thousands of servers communicate over high-capacity links, and you need to identify the “weakest link” — a small set of connections whose removal would most severely disrupt communication relative to how much traffic was severed. You want to minimize the ratio of capacity removed to traffic disrupted. That ratio is the sparsity of a cut, and finding its minimum is the Sparsest Cut problem.

This seemingly simple ratio objective is extraordinarily rich, sitting at the intersection of LP duality, metric geometry, spectral graph theory, and semidefinite programming.

Application 1 — Balanced Graph Separator

The most direct application is finding a balanced separator: a small set of edges whose removal splits \(G\) into two roughly equal halves. Define the balanced sparsity:

\[\phi_b(G)=\min_{\substack{S\subseteq V\\n/3\le|S|\le 2n/3}}\frac{|\delta(S)|}{|S|\cdot|V\setminus S|}\]

One can show \(\phi^*(G)\le\phi_b(G)\le\tfrac{9}{2}\phi^*(G)\). An \(\alpha\)-approximation to Sparsest Cut yields a \(2\alpha\)-approximation to balanced separator. Balanced separators enable divide-and-conquer on graphs, giving efficient algorithms for many NP-hard problems (see §5b).

Application 2 — Certifying Expanders

A graph \(G\) is an \(\alpha\)-expander if every \(S\) with \(|S|\le|V|/2\) satisfies \(|\delta(S)|/|S|\ge\alpha\), i.e., \(\phi(G)\ge\alpha\). Checking expansion is co-NP-hard, but an \(\alpha\)-approximation to Sparsest Cut certifies expansion within factor \(\alpha\). The quantitative relationship with the Cheeger constant \(h(G)=\min_{|S|\le n/2}|\delta(S)|/|S|\) is:

\[\frac{\phi(G)}{2}\;\le\; h(G)\;\le\;\phi(G)\]

Application 3 — Treewidth Approximation

The treewidth \(\text{tw}(G)\) (NP-hard to compute) is approximable via node-capacitated Sparsest Cut. With vertex-separator sparsity \(\phi_{\mathrm{sep}}\):

\[\phi_{\mathrm{sep}}(G)\le\text{tw}(G)+1\le O\!\left(\phi_{\mathrm{sep}}(G)\log n\right)\]

The \(O(\sqrt{\log n})\) node-capacitated Sparsest Cut approximation (Feige-Hajiaghayi-Lee 2005) gives \(\text{tw}(G)\le O(\phi_{\mathrm{sep}}^*(G)\cdot\sqrt{\log n})\), the best known polynomial-time treewidth approximation.

Application 4 — Image Segmentation (Normalized Cut)

Pixels become vertices in a similarity-weighted graph \(G=(V,E,w)\). The Normalized Cut (Shi-Malik 2000) is Uniform Sparsest Cut. The spectral relaxation uses the normalized Laplacian \(L=D^{-1/2}(D-W)D^{-1/2}\) with Cheeger's inequality:

\[\frac{\lambda_2}{2}\;\le\;\phi(G)\;\le\;\sqrt{2\lambda_2}\]

giving a \(O(\sqrt{\phi^*})\)-approximation via spectral methods — an explicit quantitative connection to Sparsest Cut.

Why is this hard?

The objective \(\phi(S)=c(\delta(S))/\mathrm{Dem}(\delta(S))\) is a ratio of two linear functions — non-linear and non-convex. NP-hard. Best known ratio: \(O(\sqrt{\log n})\) via SDP (ARV 2004). Whether \(O(1)\) is achievable remains open.

§2 Mathematical Formulation

Non-Uniform Sparsest Cut

Input: supply graph \(G=(V,E)\), capacities \(c:E\to\mathbb{R}_+\), demand pairs \((s_1,t_1),\ldots,(s_k,t_k)\), demands \(D_1,\ldots,D_k\ge 0\).

\[\phi(S)=\frac{c(\delta(S))}{\displaystyle\sum_{i:\,S\text{ separates }(s_i,t_i)}D_i}\qquad\text{Goal: }\min_{\emptyset\ne S\ne V}\phi(S)\]

Uniform Sparsest Cut

When \(D_i=1\) for every pair \((u,v)\) in the complete demand graph:

\[\phi_U(S)=\frac{c(\delta(S))}{|S|\cdot|V\setminus S|}\]

For \(|S|\le n/2\): \(\tfrac{1}{n}\cdot\tfrac{|\delta(S)|}{|S|}\le\tfrac{|\delta(S)|}{|S||V\setminus S|}\le\tfrac{2}{n}\cdot\tfrac{|\delta(S)|}{|S|}\), so expansion and Uniform Sparsest Cut differ by at most 2.

LP Relaxation & Dual

\[\begin{array}{rll}\min&\displaystyle\sum_{uv\in E}c(uv)\,d(uv)\\[4pt]\text{s.t.}&\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^k D_i\,d(s_i,t_i)=1&\text{(normalize)}\\[4pt]&d(u,v)\le d(u,w)+d(w,v)&\forall\,u,v,w\\[3pt]&d(u,v)=d(v,u)\ge 0\end{array}\qquad\text{(Dual: }\max\lambda\text{, Max Concurrent Flow)}\]
Definition — Flow-Cut Gap

The flow-cut gap is \(\min_S\phi(S)/\lambda^*\) where \(\lambda^*\) is the max concurrent flow. LP duality: \(\lambda^*\le\min_S\phi(S)\) always. General undirected graphs: gap \(=\Theta(\log k)\).

§4 Rounding I — Leighton-Rao via ℓ₁ Embeddings

Leighton-Rao (1988), reinterpreted by Linial-London-Rabinovich (1994) and Aumann-Rabani (1998), achieves an \(O(\log k)\) approximation. Two phases: solve an LP to get a fractional metric, then round via ℓ₁ embedding and cut-metric decomposition.

Phase 1 — The LP Relaxation

\[\begin{array}{rll}\min&\displaystyle\sum_{uv\in E}c(uv)\,d(uv)\\[4pt]\text{s.t.}&\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^k D_i\,d(s_i,t_i)=1,\quad d\text{ is a semi-metric on }V,\quad d\ge 0\end{array}\]

Solvable in poly time. \(\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{LP}}\le\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{INT}}\) because any integer cut \(S\) with sparsity \(\lambda\) gives feasible solution \(d=d_S/Z\), \(Z=\sum_i D_i d_S(s_i,t_i)\), with LP value \(=\lambda\).

Phase 2a — Cut Metrics and the ℓ₁ Characterization

Definition — Cut Semi-Metric

Given \(V\) and \(S\subseteq V\), the cut semi-metric \(d_S\) induced by \(S\) is:

\[d_S(u,v)=\begin{cases}1&\text{if }|S\cap\{u,v\}|=1\quad(u,v\text{ on opposite sides})\\0&\text{otherwise}\end{cases}\]

\(d_S\) is an ℓ₁ metric in \(\mathbb{R}^1\). Explicit embedding: \(f(u)=0\) if \(u\in S\), \(f(u)=1\) if \(u\notin S\), giving \(|f(u)-f(v)|=d_S(u,v)\).

Theorem — ℓ₁ = Cut Cone

A metric \((V,d)\) is isometrically embeddable in ℓ₁ (any dimension) if and only if there exists \(\lambda:2^V\to\mathbb{R}_+\) such that

\[d(u,v)=\sum_{S\subseteq V}\lambda(S)\,d_S(u,v)\qquad\forall\,u,v\in V\]
Proof (⇐) — Cut cone embeds into ℓ₁

Suppose \(d(u,v)=\sum_S\lambda(S)d_S(u,v)\). Let \(S_1,\ldots,S_h\) be the sets with \(\lambda(S_j)>0\). Embed \(V\to\mathbb{R}^h\) by:

\[f(u)=\bigl(\lambda(S_1)\,\mathbf{1}_{S_1^c}(u),\;\lambda(S_2)\,\mathbf{1}_{S_2^c}(u),\;\ldots,\;\lambda(S_h)\,\mathbf{1}_{S_h^c}(u)\bigr)\]

where \(\mathbf{1}_{S^c}(u)=0\) if \(u\in S\), else \(1\). Then:

\[\|f(u)-f(v)\|_1=\sum_{j=1}^h\lambda(S_j)|\mathbf{1}_{S_j^c}(u)-\mathbf{1}_{S_j^c}(v)|=\sum_{j=1}^h\lambda(S_j)d_{S_j}(u,v)=d(u,v)\]
■
Proof (⇒) — ℓ₁ embedding gives cut-cone decomposition

Suppose \(f:V\to\mathbb{R}^h\) with \(d(u,v)=\|f(u)-f(v)\|_1=\sum_i|u(i)-v(i)|\). Define line metric \(d_i(u,v)=|u(i)-v(i)|\). It suffices to show each \(d_i\) is in the cut cone.

Decomposing a line metric. Sort vertices: \(v_1(i)\le v_2(i)\le\cdots\le v_n(i)\). For \(j=1,\ldots,n-1\) define:

\[S_j=\{v_1,v_2,\ldots,v_j\},\qquad\lambda_i(S_j)=v_{j+1}(i)-v_j(i)\ge 0\]

and \(\lambda_i(S)=0\) for \(S\notin\{S_1,\ldots,S_{n-1}\}\). For any \(v_a,v_b\) with \(a<b\):

\[\sum_S\lambda_i(S)\,d_S(v_a,v_b)=\sum_{j=a}^{b-1}\lambda_i(S_j)\cdot 1=\sum_{j=a}^{b-1}(v_{j+1}(i)-v_j(i))=v_b(i)-v_a(i)=d_i(v_a,v_b)\]

\(d_{S_j}(v_a,v_b)=1\) exactly when \(a\le j<b\) (prefix \(S_j\) separates \(v_a\) from \(v_b\) iff \(a\le j<b\)). The sum telescopes to the coordinate difference.

Phase 2b — Bourgain’s Embedding

Theorem — Bourgain (1985)

For any \(n\)-point metric \((V,d)\) and \(k\) terminal pairs, there is a randomized poly-time contraction \(f:V\to\mathbb{R}^d\) (ℓ₁, \(d=O(\log^2 n)\)) with:

\[\|f(u)-f(v)\|_1\le d(u,v)\;\forall\,u,v\qquad\text{and}\qquad\|f(s_i)-f(t_i)\|_1\ge\frac{d(s_i,t_i)}{O(\log k)}\]

Construction: For scale \(r\) and repetition \(j\), pick random \(A_{r,j}\) (each vertex w.p. \(2^{-r}/n\)). Fréchet coord \(f_{r,j}(v)=d(v,A_{r,j})\). Pairs at distance \(\sim 2^r\) are separated by scale-\(r\) coords.

Complete LLR/AR Algorithm

SparseCutViaL1Embedding — Linial-London-Rabinovich / Aumann-RabaniRandomized \(O(\mathrm{poly}(n,k))\)
  1. Solve LP: Obtain \(d^*\) on \(V\), value \(\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{LP}}\).
  2. Embed into ℓ₁: Apply Bourgain to get contraction \(f:V\to\mathbb{R}^d\) (\(d=O(\log^2 n)\)) with distortion \(\alpha=O(\log k)\) on terminals.
  3. Extract cuts: For each dimension \(i=1,\ldots,d\): sort vertices by coord \(i\), giving \(v_{\pi(1)},\ldots,v_{\pi(n)}\). For each prefix \(h\): cut \(S_{i,h}=\{v_{\pi(1)},\ldots,v_{\pi(h)}\}\).
  4. Return best: Among all \(O(dn)\) cuts, return minimum sparsity.

Approximation Analysis

Let \(d_{\ell_1}=\sum_S z_S d_S\) (cut-cone decomp.), \(A=\sum_{uv}c(uv)d^*(uv)\), \(B=\sum_i D_i d^*(s_i,t_i)=1\):

\[A\ge\sum_{uv}c(uv)\sum_S z_S d_S(uv)=\sum_S z_S c(\delta(S))\] \[B\le\alpha\sum_S z_S\,\mathrm{Dem}(\delta(S))\] \[\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{LP}}=\frac{A}{B}\ge\frac{1}{\alpha}\min_S\frac{c(\delta(S))}{\mathrm{Dem}(\delta(S))}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\boxed{\min_S\phi(S)\le O(\log k)\cdot\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{INT}}}\]

§4b Rounding II — Multicut Reduction & Group Lemma

Before the ℓ₁ embedding framework, rounding went via Minimum Multicut: find the minimum-cost edge set disconnecting all \(k\) pairs. NP-hard, but admits \(O(\log k)\)-approximation (Garg-Vazirani-Yannakakis 1996). If we knew which pairs the optimal sparse cut separates, we could run the Multicut algorithm on just those pairs. The Group Lemma identifies them.

The Group Lemma

Group Lemma (Claim 3)

Let \(d_{\max}=\max_i d_i\). For \(\ell\ge 0\): \(A_\ell=\{i\mid d_{\max}/2^{\ell+1}<d_i\le d_{\max}/2^\ell\}\). Then \(\exists\,h\le 2\log D\) (\(D=\sum_i D_i\)) such that

\[\mathrm{dem}(A_h)\cdot\frac{d_{\max}}{2^{h+1}}\ge\frac{1}{8\log D},\qquad\mathrm{dem}(A_h)=\sum_{i\in A_h}D_i\]
Proof — step by step

1. \(\sum_i D_i d_i=1\). Groups partition all pairs: \(1=\sum_{\ell\ge0}\sum_{i\in A_\ell}D_i d_i\).

2. Set \(t=2\log D-1\). For \(\ell>t\): \(\sum_{i\in A_\ell}D_i d_i\le D/2^\ell\le 2/D\). Summing: \(\sum_{\ell>t}\sum_{i\in A_\ell}D_i d_i\le 1/4\) (for \(D\ge 8\)).

3. \(\sum_{\ell\le t}\sum_{i\in A_\ell}D_i d_i\ge 3/4\).

4. Since \(d_i<d_{\max}/2^\ell\) for \(i\in A_\ell\): \(\sum_{\ell\le t}\mathrm{dem}(A_\ell)\cdot d_{\max}/2^{\ell+1}>3/8>1/4\).

5. There are \(2\log D\) terms; by averaging one satisfies \(\mathrm{dem}(A_h)\cdot d_{\max}/2^{h+1}\ge 1/(8\log D)\). \(\square\)

Claim — Rescaled metric is Multicut-feasible

Set \(\alpha=2^{h+1}/d_{\max}\), \(\ell'(e)=\min(1,\alpha d^*(e))\). For \(i\in A_h\) and path \(p\) from \(s_i\) to \(t_i\):

\[\sum_{e\in p}\ell'(e)\ge\alpha\sum_{e\in p}d^*(e)\ge\alpha\,d^*(s_i,t_i)>\alpha\cdot\frac{d_{\max}}{2^{h+1}}=1\]

Cost: \(\sum_e c(e)\ell'(e)\le\alpha\cdot\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{LP}}\).

Complete Multicut Algorithm

SparseCutViaMulticut — \(O(\log k\cdot\log D)\) approx.\(O(\mathrm{poly}(n,k))\) + \(O(\log k)\) Multicut oracle
  1. Solve LP: Get \(d^*\), compute \(d_i=d^*(s_i,t_i)\), \(d_{\max}=\max_i d_i\).
  2. Build groups: For \(\ell=0,\ldots,2\log D\): compute \(A_\ell\) and \(\mathrm{dem}(A_\ell)\).
  3. Heavy group: Find \(h\) maximizing \(\mathrm{dem}(A_h)\cdot d_{\max}/2^{h+1}\ge 1/(8\log D)\).
  4. Rescale: \(\alpha=2^{h+1}/d_{\max}\), \(\ell'(e)=\min(1,\alpha d^*(e))\). Feasible Multicut LP for pairs \(A_h\).
  5. Round Multicut: \(O(\log k)\) rounding → cut \(E'\) with \(c(E')=O(\log k)\cdot\alpha\cdot\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{LP}}\).
  6. Return the cut induced by \(E'\).

Ratio

\[\phi(E')\le\frac{O(\log k)\cdot\alpha\cdot\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{LP}}}{\alpha/(8\log D)}=O(\log k\cdot\log D)\cdot\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{INT}}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\boxed{O(\log^2 k)\cdot\mathrm{OPT}\text{ for unit demands}}\]

§5 The \(O(\sqrt{\log n})\) ARV Algorithm

Arora, Rao, and Vazirani (2004) broke the \(O(\log n)\) barrier: \(O(\sqrt{\log n})\)-approximation for Uniform Sparsest Cut via SDP. Currently the best known result.

The ℓ₂² SDP Relaxation

Assign unit vectors \(v_u\in\mathbb{R}^n\) to each vertex. Squared distances \(\|v_i-v_j\|^2\) play the role of distances. The ℓ₂² triangle inequality (all angles acute, \(\langle v_i,v_j\rangle\ge 0\)) is a semidefinite constraint.

\[\begin{array}{rll}\min&\displaystyle\sum_{ij\in E}\|v_i-v_j\|^2\\[4pt]\text{s.t.}&\displaystyle\sum_{i,j}\|v_i-v_j\|^2=n^2,\quad\|v_i-v_j\|^2+\|v_j-v_k\|^2\ge\|v_i-v_k\|^2\;\forall\,i,j,k,\quad\|v_u\|=1\;\forall\,u\end{array}\]
Master Structure Theorem (ARV 2004)

Let \(v_1,\ldots,v_n\in\mathbb{R}^n\), \(\|v_i\|^2\le 1\), squared distances forming a metric, \(\frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i,j}\|v_i-v_j\|^2=\Omega(1)\). Then \(\exists\,S,T\subseteq V\) with \(|S|=|T|=\Omega(n)\) and

\[d(S,T)=\min_{s\in S,t\in T}\|v_s-v_t\|\ge\Omega\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{\log n}}\right)\]

Tight example: hypercube \(\{0,1\}^m\), \(n=2^m\) points in \(m=\log n\) dimensions.

ARV — Arora-Rao-Vazirani\(O(n^\omega)\) SDP + poly rounding
  1. Solve SDP: Obtain unit vectors \(\{v_u\}\), value \(\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{SDP}}\).
  2. Structure theorem: Find \(S,T\) with \(|S|=|T|=\Omega(n)\) and \(d(S,T)\ge\Omega(1/\sqrt{\log n})\).
  3. Random projection: Sample \(\mathbf{g}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I_n)\). Compute \(p_v=\langle\mathbf{g},v_v\rangle\) for each \(v\).
  4. Threshold cut: Choose \(\theta\) uniformly between projected medians of \(S\) and \(T\). Output \(\{v:p_v\le\theta\}\).
  5. Repeat \(O(\log n)\) times; return minimum sparsity cut.

The rounding loses \(O(\sqrt{\log n})\) relative to the SDP, giving an overall \(O(\sqrt{\log n})\)-approximation. The SDP integrality gap is also \(\Omega(\sqrt{\log n})\) (tight, hypercube construction).

§5b Balanced Separator — Connection to Sparsest Cut

Definition — Balanced Separator

A \((\beta,f)\)-balanced separator of \(G=(V,E)\) is \(F\subseteq E\) such that every connected component of \(G-F\) has \(\le\beta n\) vertices and \(|F|\le f(n)\). The canonical case is \(\beta=2/3\).

Formal Reduction from Sparsest Cut

\[\phi_b(G)=\min_{\substack{S\subseteq V\\n/3\le|S|\le 2n/3}}\frac{|\delta(S)|}{|S||V\setminus S|},\qquad\phi^*(G)\le\phi_b(G)\le\tfrac{9}{2}\,\phi^*(G)\]

The right inequality: if optimal cut \(S^*\) is unbalanced (\(|S^*|<n/3\)), extend it by including the neighboring component until balanced; this multiplies sparsity by at most \(9/2\).

Theorem — Balanced Separator from Sparsest Cut (Leighton-Rao 1988)

Given an \(\alpha\)-approximation \(\mathcal{A}\) for Sparsest Cut, the algorithm below finds a \((2/3)\)-balanced separator \(F\) with \(|F|\le O(\alpha\cdot\phi^*(G)\cdot n)\).

BalancedSeparator via Sparsest Cut\(O(T_{\mathcal{A}})\) per level, \(O(\log n)\) levels
  1. Sparse cut: Run \(\mathcal{A}\) on \(G\) to get cut \((S,V\setminus S)\) with sparsity \(\le\alpha\cdot\phi^*(G)\).
  2. Check balance: If \(n/3\le|S|\le 2n/3\), output \(\delta(S)\).
  3. Re-balance: If \(|S|<n/3\), move vertices from the largest neighboring component into \(S\) until balanced.
  4. Output the balanced cut edges \(\delta(S')\).

Recursive Application

Applying recursively gives a separator tree of depth \(O(\log n)\). Total edges cut across all levels:

§6 Key Proofs & Analysis

LP is a Relaxation

Proof

Given integer cut \(S\) with sparsity \(\lambda\), set \(d=d_S/Z\), \(Z=\sum_i D_i d_S(s_i,t_i)\). Normalization holds, triangle inequality holds, LP value \(=c(\delta(S))/Z=\lambda\). So \(\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{LP}}\le\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{INT}}\).

■

LP is Tight on Trees

Proof — Lemma 6

LP-feasible \(d\), value \(\lambda\). On a tree \(d(s_i,t_i)=\sum_{e\in P_i}d(e)\). Let \(D(e)=\sum_{i:e\in P_i}D_i\). Then:

\[\lambda=\frac{\sum_e c(e)d(e)}{\sum_e d(e)D(e)}\ge\min_e\frac{c(e)}{D(e)}=\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{INT}}\]

So \(\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{LP}}=\mathrm{OPT}_{\mathrm{INT}}\) on trees (no integrality gap).

■

Flow-Cut Gap is \(O(\log k)\)

Proof Sketch — Theorem 11

Embed \((V,d^*)\) into ℓ₁ with distortion \(\alpha=O(\log k)\) as a contraction; write \(\ell_1\) metric as \(\sum_S z_S d_S\). Then:

\[\lambda=\frac{\sum_e c(e)d^*(e)}{\sum_i D_i d^*(s_i,t_i)}\ge\frac{\sum_S z_S c(\delta(S))}{\alpha\sum_S z_S\mathrm{Dem}(\delta(S))}\ge\frac{1}{\alpha}\min_S\phi(S)\]

So flow-cut gap \(\le O(\log k)\).

§7 Research Timeline & Open Problems

1988 — Leighton & Rao
\(O(\log n)\) approximation. Region growing, LP duality, expander lower bound \(\Omega(\log n)\).
1994–1998 — LLR, Aumann-Rabani
\(O(\log k)\) via metric embeddings. Connected flow-cut gap to ℓ₁ embeddability.
2004 — Arora, Rao, Vazirani
\(O(\sqrt{\log n})\) via SDP. First sub-logarithmic approximation. Master Structure Theorem.
2005 — Feige, Hajiaghayi, Lee
Node-capacitated Sparsest Cut. \(O(\sqrt{\log n})\)-approx. for treewidth via line embeddings.
2007–2008 — Arora, Lee, Naor
\(O(\sqrt{\log n\log\log n})\) for non-uniform via Fréchet embeddings of negative-type metrics.
2010s — SDP Gap Lower Bound
ARV SDP integrality gap is \(\Omega(\sqrt{\log n})\) (tight). Rules out \(O(1)\) via ARV SDP alone.

Open Problems

  • Sub-\(\sqrt{\log n}\) approximation: SDP gap rules out improvement via ARV-style SDPs. Whether \(O(1)\) is achievable by other methods is completely open.
  • Directed graphs: Flow-cut gap can be \(\Omega(n^{11/27})\) (Chuzhoy-Khanna 2009), with near-polynomial hardness.
  • Special graph families: Series-parallel graphs have gap exactly 2 (tight). Tight constants for other minor-closed families remain open.
  • SDP hierarchies: Do Lasserre or Sherali-Adams hierarchies beat \(O(\sqrt{\log n})\)?

§9 Conclusion

The Sparsest Cut problem has driven the development of deep tools across combinatorial optimization, metric geometry, and semidefinite programming over three decades. Starting from the ratio objective \(\phi(S)=c(\delta(S))/\mathrm{Dem}(\delta(S))\), the problem has resisted exact algorithms and pushed researchers toward increasingly sophisticated techniques.

The journey — from LP relaxation to ℓ₁ embeddings (O(log k)) to SDP (O(√log n)) — illustrates a recurring pattern: the right geometric relaxation unlocks exponential improvements. The connection to balanced separators shows that every improvement in Sparsest Cut has immediate algorithmic consequences across graph algorithms. The ℓ₁ = cut-cone characterization, proved in full above, is the foundational insight tying metric geometry to graph cuts.

The Big Picture

Sparsest Cut is a lens through which we study the geometry of graphs. The SDP integrality gap of \(\Omega(\sqrt{\log n})\) shows the ARV approach is tight — any future improvement requires genuinely new mathematical ideas beyond SDP relaxations.
